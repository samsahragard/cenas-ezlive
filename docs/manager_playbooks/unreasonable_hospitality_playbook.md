# Distilled Playbook: Unreasonable Hospitality for Store Managers
*Based on Unreasonable Hospitality: The Remarkable Power of Giving People More Than They Expect by Will Guidara*

This playbook compiles actionable principles for GMs, KMs, and FOH Managers to create a hospitality-first culture, manage team dynamics, and deliver color-grade guest experiences at Cena's Kitchen.

---

### 1. Service is Black and White; Hospitality is Color
* **Logic**: Service is the technical delivery of the job with competence and efficiency (e.g., getting the correct order to the correct table). Hospitality is the emotional feeling—making the guest feel welcome, seen, and valued. Doing your job is service; connecting with the human is hospitality.
* **Manager Action**: Train staff that technical execution is only the baseline. Challenge them to add "color" to every interaction through warm eye contact, genuine smiles, calling guests by name, and making authentic personal connections.

### 2. Making Magic (The Nobility of Service)
* **Logic**: Hospitality is a noble calling. A restaurant can create a magical, positive oasis where the outside world falls away, giving guests a brief moment of consolation during hard times or extra joy during celebrations.
* **Manager Action**: Motivate your team by reminding them of the nobility of service. Encourage them to see themselves as magic-makers who help guests celebrate life, recover from difficult days, or find respite.

### 3. The Power of Intention (Precision Planning)
* **Logic**: Intention means every decision—from the most significant to the most mundane—matters. To lead with intentionality means doing things thoughtfully, with clear purpose and an eye on the desired result, rather than leading by default.
* **Manager Action**: Plan shifts and weekly rosters with deliberate focus. Do not leave floor coverage or side-work to chance. Establish clear, non-negotiable standards for shift readiness and stick to them.

### 4. Enthusiasm is Contagious (Leading the Energy)
* **Logic**: A leader's energy is a wave that picks up the team. Let your positive energy impact the team, rather than letting their stress, tiredness, or cynicism drag you down.
* **Manager Action**: Model high energy and optimism during pre-shift briefings. Pump up the team, greet them warmly, and use your presence to shift the mood on the floor.

### 5. Trust and Ownership (Empowering the Team)
* **Logic**: Delegating authority and trusting employees builds a sense of ownership, making them work harder and care more, especially when the manager is not looking.
* **Manager Action**: Empower lead servers and cooks to make decisions (like handling guest complaints, comping items, or check adjustments). Show them you trust their judgment and support their decisions.

### 6. Language Creates Culture (Shared Shorthand)
* **Logic**: Establishing a shared internal vocabulary makes core cultural concepts easy to understand, recall, and teach.
* **Manager Action**: Adopt and use unified terms on shifts (e.g., "Constant, gentle pressure", "Be the swan", "Make the charitable assumption") to reinforce cultural values during coaching.

### 7. Restaurant-Smart vs. Corporate-Smart (Systemized Autonomy)
* **Logic**: Restaurant-smart is about floor autonomy, creativity, and customer care. Corporate-smart is about backend systems, budgets, and cost controls. True success requires balancing both—using systems to free up creativity.
* **Manager Action**: Use checklists, inventory controls, and scheduling tools diligently (corporate-smart) so that you and your team have the breathing room to be creative and hospitable on the floor (restaurant-smart).

### 8. Pursuing a True Partnership (FOH and BOH Alignment)
* **Logic**: Operational success requires a unified front and synergy between FOH (front of house) management and BOH (back of house/kitchen) management.
* **Manager Action**: Align daily with the Kitchen Manager/Chef. Resolve any friction privately, show mutual respect on the floor, and present a collaborative front to the entire team.

### 9. Setting Expectations (Defining "Right")
* **Logic**: Clear expectations prevent frustration. Define exactly what "right" looks like for every role and task, removing ambiguity.
* **Manager Action**: Inspect and define the standard for tasks (e.g., table settings, prep checks). Don't just say "clean the station"; show them exactly what a clean station looks like.

### 10. Challenging Standard Norms (Hiring for Passion)
* **Logic**: Focus on natural curiosity, integrity, and warmth during recruiting. Hire for passion and emotional fit over simple resume checklists.
* **Manager Action**: During interviews, ask candidates about their interest in hospitality and what makes them feel good about taking care of others. Seek candidates who align with a hospitality-first culture.

### 11. Working with Purpose (The "Why" Behind the "What")
* **Logic**: A team performs best when they know *why* they are doing the work, not just *what* they are doing. Connect daily tasks to a larger shared mission.
* **Manager Action**: Explain the "why" behind policies, menu changes, or station setups during briefings, connecting them to guest experience or team success.

### 12. Culture of Collaboration (Gathering Input)
* **Logic**: Great ideas can come from anyone. Getting team input and buy-in on decisions makes them partners in execution.
* **Manager Action**: Ask your staff for feedback on shift flow, layout, or prep methods. Incorporate their good ideas into operations to build buy-in.

### 13. Pushing Toward Excellence (Attention to Detail)
* **Logic**: Excellence is the result of continuous attention to detail. Never ignore minor deviations from standards; high quality requires rigorous focus.
* **Manager Action**: Do walk-throughs to check table settings, plate presentations, and cleaning. Coach staff immediately when standard details are missed.

### 14. Relationships Are Simple, But Hard (Individual Care)
* **Logic**: Deep individual relationships are built by listening carefully and showing genuine care, which requires effort and consistency.
* **Manager Action**: Spend time talking with employees individually. Know their interests, career goals, and personal details, and check in on them regularly.

### 15. Leveraging Affirmation (Celebrating Wins)
* **Logic**: Praise is a powerful motivator. Recognizing and celebrating small wins builds positive momentum and reinforces good behavior.
* **Manager Action**: Make a point to praise at least three employees on every shift for specific good deeds or great execution. Celebrate these wins publicly.

### 16. Restoring Balance (Burnout Prevention)
* **Logic**: Peak performance requires rest. Managing stress and preventing burnout among managers and team members protects the customer experience.
* **Manager Action**: Monitor staff workloads and overtime. Ensure shifts are balanced and that employees have adequate rest between shifts to prevent burnout.

### 17. The Best Offense Is Offense (Proactive Hospitality)
* **Logic**: Proactive hospitality is far more effective than defensive service recovery. Anticipate guest needs and solve problems before they complain.
* **Manager Action**: Notice long ticket times or guest frowns early. Proactively visit tables to apologize and offer solutions before they ask to speak to a manager.

### 18. Earning Informality (Warm, Personal Connections)
* **Logic**: True hospitality feels personal and relaxed, not stiff or formal. You earn the right to be casual by first proving you are highly competent.
* **Manager Action**: Ensure technical service (greeting speed, order accuracy) is perfect, then encourage servers to show their personality and build warm, friendly connections.

### 19. Learning to Be Unreasonable (Bespoke Gestures)
* **Logic**: Going completely over-the-top to make people feel special ("unreasonable hospitality") creates lifelong fans and changes the business game.
* **Manager Action**: Look for opportunities to surprise guests with unexpected kindness (e.g., sending a free sample of their favorite dish, giving kids a small toy, writing a personalized note).

### 20. Improvisational Hospitality (Dynamic Service)
* **Logic**: Tailor guest experiences in real-time based on verbal and non-verbal cues. Move away from generic scripts to bespoke service.
* **Manager Action**: Train staff to listen for details (e.g., birthday mentions, traveling from out of town) and adapt the table experience dynamically to celebrate those details.

### 21. Scaling a Culture (Defending Core Values)
* **Logic**: As a restaurant grows or expands to multiple stores, the core culture must be intentionally defended, taught, and prioritized by the leadership.
* **Manager Action**: Act as a custodian of the restaurant's culture. Ensure new hires are fully onboarded into company values, not just operational tasks.

### 22. Back to Basics (Core Human Element)
* **Logic**: When systems become complex or operations feel overwhelming, return to the core human element—making people feel welcomed and cared for.
* **Manager Action**: During chaotic shifts, strip away complexity and focus on the basics: greet people warmly, keep food hot, and support your team.
